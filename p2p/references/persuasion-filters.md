# P2P Persuasion Filters

Run through EVERY piece of copy after the first draft. Your first draft is never the final product.

## The 6 Editing Filters

### Filter 1: AIDA Check (Reverse-Engineered)

Check in reverse order:
1. **Action:** Is there a clear CTA? Does the reader know exactly what to do?
2. **Desire:** Is desire created for the action? Do they WANT to do it?
3. **Interest:** Is interest piqued? Are they curious about the solution?
4. **Attention:** Does the opening grab attention? Would they stop scrolling?

These don't have to follow rigid order in copy -- some happen simultaneously. But all four must be present.

### Filter 2: 10-Year-Old Test

Read your copy and ask: "Would a 10-year-old understand this?"

Look for:
- Words too complex (replace with simpler alternatives)
- Sentences not clear (break them down)
- Concepts too difficult to grasp quickly (explain differently)

"Dumbing your copy down is not bad -- it's good." More people understand it = more conversions.

### Filter 3: Length/Variety Check (Peaks & Valleys)

- Avoid walls of text
- Avoid long sentences as much as possible
- NEVER use multiple long sentences in a row
- Mix long with short -- copy should look like "a mountain with peaks and valleys"
- If long section is needed, break it with connecting words

### Filter 4: Abstract vs Concrete Check

Ask: "If 100 people read this, would they ALL picture the SAME thing?" If not --> too abstract.

| Abstract (BAD) | Concrete (GOOD) |
|---|---|
| "Achieve financial freedom" | "Have $10,436.26 deposited into your bank account every month" |
| "Feel confident in social situations" | "Feel at ease at your friend's upcoming backyard barbecue" |
| "Don't look overweight" | "Scroll through your Instagram and see a person who radiates health" |
| "Signs of aging" | "Wrinkles around your eyes" |
| "Love of your life" | Specific description of what that looks like in daily life |
| "Make you confident at work" | "Lead Monday morning meetings knowing colleagues focus on your ideas, not your size" |

Replace every abstract claim with concrete, tangible, experiential language.

### Filter 5: Connecting Words Check

Are there enough transitions between ideas and paragraphs?

Examples: "Here's the truth," "The fact is," "And worse yet," "Truth is," "Here's why," "But here's the thing," "In other words," "Put simply," "Now you might be wondering"

These create visual variety, continuity, and improve retention rate.

### Filter 6: Blair Warren's One-Sentence Persuasion

> "People will do anything for those who encourage their dreams, justify their failures, allay their fears, confirm their suspicions, and help them throw rocks at their enemies."

Check if your copy includes ALL 5 elements:

#### 1. Encourage Their Dreams
"It is definitely possible for you to achieve this."
Tell them they are capable. Give them hope.

#### 2. Justify Their Failures
"The reason you haven't accomplished this yet is because of [external factor]. It's not your fault."
Taking pressure off someone's shoulders makes your message way more powerful. One of the most powerful things you can say in copy.

#### 3. Allay Their Fears
Reduce fears about the process, outcome, or their ability.
Naturally accomplished when you justify failures and encourage dreams.

#### 4. Confirm Their Suspicions
People have things they suspect and WANT to be true. Confirm those suspicions.
"You're right -- those restrictive diets DON'T work. Here's why..."

#### 5. Help Them Throw Rocks at Their Enemies
Create a common enemy. "There's no better way to bond with people than having a common enemy."
The enemy can be: a diet, a person, a limitation, a challenge, the industry, the conventional path.
"It turns from 'me against the world' into 'us against the world.'"

## Additional P2P-Specific Checks

After the 6 filters, also verify:

- [ ] **Duality present?** Does the copy contain at least one conflict/duality?
- [ ] **Depth check:** Does it reach internal/philosophical problem level (not just external)?
- [ ] **Signature solution:** Is it properly named (avoids category words like "diet," "course")?
- [ ] **Repetition:** Is the core message repeated effectively (different wording, same point)?
- [ ] **"You" usage:** Is "you" used for 1-on-1 communication feel?
- [ ] **Problem-first:** Does it lead with the problem before presenting the solution?
- [ ] **Product = vehicle:** Is the product positioned as the vehicle for the solution, not the solution itself?
